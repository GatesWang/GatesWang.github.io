Claim: Any bijection from $S$ to $S$ where $S = \{0,1,2,3\}$ can be written as a composition of three mappings $f$, $g$, and $h$
The notation $f\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{1,2,3,0\}$ means the following
$0 \rightarrow 1$
$1 \rightarrow 2$
$2 \rightarrow 3$
$3 \rightarrow 0$
We can similarly define $g\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{0,2,3,1\}$ and $h\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{0,1,3,2\}$


Proof:
Notice that applying $f\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{1,2,3,0\}$ is equivalent to shifting all the digits to the left by one place.
Moreover, applying $f\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{1,2,3,0\}$ repeatedly is the same as shifting over repeatedly.
$f\Bigg(f\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big)\Bigg) = f\Big(\{1,2,3,0\}\Big)$ because

$0 \rightarrow 1$
$1 \rightarrow 2$
$2 \rightarrow 3$
$3 \rightarrow 0$

We then use the same mapping to evaluate $f\Big(\{1,2,3,0\}\Big) = \{2,3,0,1\}$ because
$0 \rightarrow 1$
$1 \rightarrow 2$
$2 \rightarrow 3$
$3 \rightarrow 0$

$g\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{0,2,3,1\}$ fixes the leftmost digit in place and shifts the other digits to the left.
$h\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big) = \{0,1,3,2\}$ fixes the two leftmost digits in place and shifts the other digits to the left.

Hence, any mapping from S to S can be written using $f\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big)$, $g\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big)$, and $h\Big(\{0,1,2,3\}\Big)$.
Take for example, the mapping $\{0,1,2,3\} \rightarrow \{1,0,3,2\}$
$f(\{0,1,2,3\}) = \{1,2,3,0\}$
$g\big(f(\{0,1,2,3\})\big) = \{1,3,0,2\}$
$g\Big(g\big(f(\{0,1,2,3\})\big)\Big) = \{1,0,2,3\}$
$h\Bigg(g\Big(g\big(f(\{0,1,2,3\})\big)\Big)\Bigg) = \{1,0,3,2\}$
